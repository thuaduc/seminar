\begin{abstract}
  Large language models have moved from autocompleting single functions to
  autonomously resolving real software issues, and this report surveys the agents
  that do so through a single lens: a five-stage \emph{agent pipeline} (intent
  capture, planning, code generation, execution, refinement). Our thesis is that
  planning and refinement, usually studied in isolation, together form a control
  layer over generation, but that this layer exists only where some signal
  \emph{not produced by the generator itself} can contradict the model. We survey
  three fronts against that pipeline: end-to-end agents (SWE-agent, Agentless,
  AutoCodeRover, OpenHands, Devin) and the patterns they share; planning and
  search, where we argue planning contributes \emph{grounding} rather than
  deliberation; and execution feedback, whose mechanisms we sort by what closes
  their loop, an authoritative oracle, a learned or generated proxy, or the
  generator's own critique. Only the first can surprise the model; the second is
  bounded by the proxy's fidelity; the third, once budget-normalised, is an open
  loop wearing a control loop's clothes. The framing locates the pipeline's two
  weaknesses at the two stages with no oracle behind them: intent capture, which no
  surveyed agent instruments, and test-based validation, whose oracle is weak
  enough to be gamed. We close on evaluation realism and cost.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
Coding with a language model used to mean accepting an autocomplete suggestion.
Increasingly it means describing a change in plain English and letting a system
find the right files, edit them, run the tests, and iterate until they pass. The
appeal and the worry are the same fact: the developer states an intent and reviews
an outcome, while the steps in between (reading the codebase, forming a
hypothesis, running the debugger) are delegated to the model. The measured
progress is real. In late~2023 there were no agents to speak of: the strongest
result on SWE-bench, a benchmark of real GitHub issues, came from
retrieval-augmented prompting of a frontier model and resolved under 2\% of
them~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}. Two years later agents resolve most of the
human-verified split. This report asks what produced that jump, and what it still
does not deliver.

\paragraph{Background and Related Surveys.}
Existing surveys catalogue agents in software engineering~\cite{wang2024agentsse}
and LLM-based code-generation agents~\cite{dong2025surveycodegen}, organised
largely by system or by capability. This report instead organises three active
research fronts (end-to-end agents, planning and search, and execution-feedback
refinement) around the pipeline stages they instantiate, which lets us compare
systems that describe themselves in incompatible vocabularies.

\paragraph{Scope and Contribution.}
We cover agents whose output is a code change: repository-level issue
resolution, automated program repair, and function-level synthesis, drawing on
the 2023--2026 literature. Editor autocompletion and code search lie outside this
scope, as do multi-agent \emph{role-play} frameworks that simulate a development
team (ChatDev~\cite{qian2024chatdev}, MetaGPT~\cite{hong2024metagpt}), whose
contribution is an organisational metaphor rather than a mechanism for grounding
a patch. The exclusion does not extend to
multi-agent \emph{search and decomposition} pipelines such as
SWE-Search~\cite{antoniades2024swesearch}: these produce code changes and are in
scope, and we treat them below as variants of the search strategies of
\S\ref{sec:planning} rather than as a separate architectural family.
The report contributes, first, the
agent pipeline as a shared vocabulary for these systems
(\S\ref{sec:pipeline}); second, a survey of the three fronts read against that
pipeline (\S\ref{sec:e2e}--\S\ref{sec:refinement}); and third, a consolidation
of what the fronts share and where they are jointly stuck
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}), drawing on 2025--2026 evidence about benchmark
contamination and cost that postdates most existing surveys.

\section{The Agent Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

\subsection{Five Stages}

Papers on autonomous coding agents describe their systems in their own terms:
phases, modules, roles, loops. Underneath, nearly all do the same five things.
Naming those stages gives a frame for comparing systems that otherwise share no
vocabulary. The pipeline is not a new architecture but a lens on existing ones,
and its value is that it makes one question askable of every system at once: at
each stage, what can tell the model it is wrong? The stages loosely rhyme with the
classical waterfall phases, but the loop breaks the resemblance: an agent
traverses all five in seconds and repeats them many times per task, and the cycle
is closed by a test runner rather than a review meeting. The closer analogue is
the developer's inner loop of edit, run, and debug, with planning and localisation
folded in.

The agent pipeline (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}) consists of five
stages forming a loop: Intent Capture \textrightarrow{} Planning
\textrightarrow{} Code Generation \textrightarrow{} Execution
\textrightarrow{} Refinement. Execution feedback drives refinement, which may
trigger re-planning or re-generation. Adjacent stages exchange structured
artefacts: natural-language intent and repository context flow into plans and
candidate patches; generated code is run against test oracles; failures and
traces feed back into replanning or repair.

\subsection{Mapping to Survey Topics}

What makes the five-stage division load-bearing for the argument, rather than for
the table of contents, is what each stage \emph{does}. Read as a control system,
intent capture is the uncontrolled input; generation is the plant; execution is
the oracle; planning and refinement are the control layer tethering the plant to
the oracle. The pipeline is therefore a map of where oracles are and are not, and
the two stages with none behind them (intent capture, and validation whose oracle
is a weak test suite) turn out to be exactly the two places the field is stuck
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}). Each of the three main sections corresponds to one or
more stages.
\S\ref{sec:e2e} covers the full pipeline as an integrated system, including
action interfaces for code generation (ACI, code-as-action, diff-based edits);
\S\ref{sec:planning} treats intent capture (\S\ref{sec:intent}) together with
planning, candidate search, and the plan--generate interface, since in practice
an agent operationalises intent and plans over it in the same breath;
\S\ref{sec:refinement} covers execution environments, test-driven feedback, and
the refinement loop (including fault-specific self-repair). Code generation is
deliberately not given its own section: no surveyed system treats emitting
tokens as a separable problem, and the design decisions that govern it (action
space, edit format, candidate sampling) belong to the stages on either side of
it. It is therefore distributed across \S\ref{sec:e2e} and
\S\ref{sec:planning}. Cross-cutting concerns (evaluation, context management)
are consolidated in the Discussion.



\section{End-to-End Autonomous Software Engineering Agents}
\label{sec:e2e}

Before examining individual stages, we survey systems that orchestrate the
\emph{entire} pipeline, accepting a natural-language issue and producing a tested
code change with minimal human intervention. These end-to-end agents are the
setting in which planning (\S\ref{sec:planning}) and feedback-driven refinement
(\S\ref{sec:refinement}) operate. We identify the architectural patterns common to
them (\S\ref{sec:e2e-arch}), survey five systems spanning the design space from
fully autonomous to deliberately non-agentic (\S\ref{sec:e2e-systems}), and
examine how they are evaluated (\S\ref{sec:e2e-eval}).

\subsection{Architecture and Orchestration}
\label{sec:e2e-arch}

Despite surface-level diversity, autonomous SE agents converge on three patterns,
governing how they perceive the environment, what actions they may take, and how
they manage repository-scale context.

\paragraph{The observe--think--act loop.}
The dominant paradigm is a ReAct-style~\cite{yao2023react} loop alternating
between (i)~observing the environment (file contents, test output, error traces),
(ii)~reasoning about the next step, and (iii)~executing an action via a tool call.
SWE-agent~\cite{yang2024sweagent} and OpenHands~\cite{wang2024openhands} both
instantiate it, differing mainly in the \emph{action space} exposed to the model.

\paragraph{The action interface.}
Yang et al.~\cite{yang2024sweagent} argue that LLMs are a ``new category of end
users'' needing a purpose-built \emph{agent--computer interface} (ACI) rather than
raw shell access. SWE-agent's comprises a windowed file viewer (100~lines), a
linter-guarded \texttt{edit} command, summarised search results, and compressed
observation history, and its ablations show each component contributes measurably:
removing the linter costs 3~percentage points, switching to iterative search
6~pp, and showing full files instead of a 100-line window 5.3~pp on Lite. The
opposite design is \emph{code-as-action}: OpenHands adopts the CodeAct
paradigm~\cite{wang2024codeact}, consolidating every action into executable Python
and bash inside a Docker sandbox, which maximises flexibility at the cost of a
larger action space to navigate.

\paragraph{Context management.}
Repository-level tasks easily exceed LLM context windows, and each system
mitigates differently. SWE-agent collapses observations older than five turns.
AutoCodeRover~\cite{zhang2024autocoderover} parses the codebase into an AST and
exposes search APIs that expand context coarse-to-fine, \texttt{search\_class}
returning a class skeleton of signatures and \texttt{search\_method\_in\_class}
the full body of one method once the agent has narrowed to it.
Agentless~\cite{xia2024agentless} builds a hierarchical repository skeleton and
retrieves candidate files by embedding before prompting.

\subsection{Representative Systems}
\label{sec:e2e-systems}

We survey five prominent systems that span the design space.
Table~\ref{tab:e2e-systems} provides a comparative overview.

\paragraph{SWE-agent~\cite{yang2024sweagent}.}
SWE-agent wraps a GPT-4 Turbo backbone in a custom ACI with specialised file
navigation, editing, and search commands, achieving 12.47\% on the full SWE-bench
test set and 18.00\% on Lite when first published. Its trajectory is typical:
reproduce the issue, localise via \texttt{find\_file}/\texttt{search\_dir}, then
enter an edit--execute loop.
Runs average \$1.67 per instance on Lite, but the distribution is bimodal:
successful trajectories terminate quickly (a median of 12~steps, \$1.21), whereas
failures run to the step limit before giving up (a mean of 21~steps,
\$2.52).\footnote{The two statistics are as reported by the authors and are not
directly comparable: the success figure is a median and the failure figure a
mean. The qualitative point (failures cost more than successes, so a low average
conceals an expensive tail) does not depend on the choice.}

\paragraph{Agentless~\cite{xia2024agentless}.}
Agentless challenges the need for agentic autonomy, replacing the interactive loop
with a fixed three-phase pipeline:
\emph{localise}~$\to$~\emph{repair}~$\to$~\emph{validate}. Localisation narrows
hierarchically (files~$\to$~classes/functions~$\to$~edit locations) by combining
LLM prompting with embedding retrieval. Repair samples 40~candidate patches per
bug in a \emph{search/replace} diff format that anchors each edit to the
surrounding source text rather than to a line number, which the authors adopt
because it is cheaper and less prone to hallucination than emitting whole files.
Validation selects among them by regression testing, LLM-generated reproduction
tests, and majority voting over AST-normalised candidates. On GPT-4o it resolved
32.00\% of Lite at \$0.70 per problem, the best open-source figure at the time and
a strong non-agentic baseline.\footnote{These are the figures of the revised
release (Agentless~1.5, October~2024), carried in Table~\ref{tab:e2e-systems}; the
originally published version reported 27.33\% at \$0.34 on the same backbone. The
comparison below is unaffected, since the matched-backbone baselines come from the
same revision.} OpenAI subsequently adopted Agentless as the scaffold for
reporting GPT-4o and o1 results on Verified, describing it as the best-performing
open-source scaffold available~\cite{openai2024verified}.

\paragraph{AutoCodeRover~\cite{zhang2024autocoderover}.}
AutoCodeRover operates on program representations rather than raw files, parsing
the codebase into an AST and exposing structure-aware search APIs; a stratified
retrieval process expands context from issue keywords through successive search
rounds. Where a test suite exists, Spectrum-Based Fault Localisation (SBFL)
augments the search with suspiciousness scores. On a GPT-4 backbone it resolved
19.0\% of Lite and 12.4\% of Full in a single pass. Two extensions raise this
without changing the backbone: SBFL lifts Lite to 22\%, uniquely solving
7~instances no other configuration reaches, while pass@3 over three sampled runs
gives 26.0\% on Lite and 18.0\% on Full, at \$1.30 per task against \$0.43 at
pass@1.

\paragraph{OpenHands~\cite{wang2024openhands}.}
Formerly OpenDevin, OpenHands is an open-source platform (ICLR~2025) built around
the CodeAct agent. Each session runs in a Docker container with a bash shell,
IPython server, and browser, and a generalist agent may delegate to specialised
ones. CodeAct~v1.8 with Claude~3.5~Sonnet achieves 26.0\% on Lite; a later
submission reaches 60.6\% on Verified with a single rollout and 66.4\% when five
trajectories are reranked by a dedicated critic
model~\cite{openhands2025verified}.

\paragraph{Devin (Cognition)~\cite{cognition2024devin}.}
Devin is a proprietary cloud-hosted agent separating a \emph{Brain} (reasoning)
from a \emph{DevBox} (execution VM with shell, IDE, and browser), with a
persistent knowledge store for cross-session memory. It is the one system surveyed
here with no accompanying paper: the only primary source is a launch blog post
reporting 13.86\% resolved on SWE-bench. That is an order of magnitude above the
1.96\% retrieval-augmented baseline and is the result that launched the agentic
turn, but it was measured on a random 25\% subset, with no released trajectories
or harness, and has never been independently reproduced. The one longitudinal
external assessment, a month-long trial by Answer.AI, found Devin completed 3~of
20~tasks with 14~outright failures~\cite{answerai2025devin}. We include Devin for
its architectural influence, not as a comparable data point; its row in
Table~\ref{tab:e2e-systems} is a vendor claim, not a measurement.

Taken together, these five expose a trade-off between agentic flexibility and
pipeline efficiency. Agentless shows that a fixed localise--repair--validate
pipeline can outperform interactive agents on Lite at lower cost, challenging the
assumption that autonomy is necessary for strong results. The claim needs care,
because the rows of Table~\ref{tab:e2e-systems} do not share a backbone (Agentless
runs on GPT-4o, SWE-agent's published figure on GPT-4~Turbo) and
\S\ref{sec:e2e-eval} shows that swapping the backbone alone moves a resolve rate
by ten points. It survives only because Agentless's authors also report the
matched comparison, running the agentic baselines on the same GPT-4o backbone,
where the gap persists~\cite{xia2024agentless}. It is that matched comparison, not
the cross-backbone table, that carries the argument. Among agentic designs,
interface and context strategy matter as much as the backbone: SWE-agent's ACI
ablations, OpenHands's CodeAct action space, and AutoCodeRover's AST search APIs
each shift resolve rates by several percentage points at comparable model
capacity.

\subsection{Full-Pipeline Evaluation}
\label{sec:e2e-eval}

\paragraph{SWE-bench.}
The primary evaluation instrument is SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}:
2{,}294~merged pull requests scraped from 12~Python repositories, retaining only
PRs that both resolve a linked GitHub issue and modify the test suite. The two
filters do different work. The test-suite filter supplies the grading oracle; the
issue-linkage filter supplies the task's \emph{intent}, and is the reason an agent
has any natural-language input at all. An agent receives the repository snapshot
from \emph{before} the merge together with the issue text; its patch is scored
against \emph{fail-to-pass} tests (which must flip) and \emph{pass-to-pass} tests
(which must not regress). The headline metric throughout is the \emph{resolve
rate}: the percentage of instances where both sets pass on the first submitted
patch (pass@1).

\paragraph{Terminology and comparability.}
``SWE-bench'' denotes a family of task sets rather than a single benchmark
(Table~\ref{tab:swebench-splits}). Two distinctions warrant emphasis. Verified is
the most carefully \emph{curated} split, not the most demanding one: human
screening removed underspecified issues and unfair tests, so its resolve rates run
systematically higher than Full's or Lite's, and scores are not comparable across
splits. Second, \emph{easy}, \emph{medium}, and \emph{hard} are not splits but
annotator difficulty tiers within Verified, defined by the time a human engineer
was estimated to require ($<$15~min, 15~min--1~h, $>$1~h)~\cite{ganhotra2025difficulty}.

\paragraph{Leaderboard trajectory.}
At launch, retrieval-augmented prompting of a frontier model scored barely above
zero~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}: the models could write plausible patches but had
no way to find the right file or check their own work. The first agentic
scaffolds (SWE-agent, AutoCodeRover, Devin) moved the frontier by an order of
magnitude within months without any change to the underlying model, purely by
supplying tools, a repository to navigate, and tests to run against. The 2024
scaffolding race that followed exposed how much of that gain came from
scaffolding rather than reasoning capacity, since a deliberately simple
localise-then-repair pipeline such as Agentless stayed competitive with far more
elaborate agentic loops.

A headline resolve rate hides where that progress actually landed.
Figure~\ref{fig:swebench-trajectory} therefore splits Verified by the difficulty
its human annotators assigned, tracking nine leaderboard systems from
April~2024 to March~2025.

\section{Planning and Search Strategies}
\label{sec:planning}

Planning is the stage in which an agent turns a vague intent into an operational
hypothesis about \emph{what} code should change and \emph{how}. In practice it is
rarely a single up-front step: agents plan while reading issue reports, searching
repositories, choosing files, sampling patches, and revising after failed tests.
Planning is therefore best understood as a control layer over generation rather
than a document written before generation begins. This section treats intent
capture (\S\ref{sec:intent}), plan representations, search, and the
plan--generate interface. Unlike \S\ref{sec:e2e}, the mechanisms below are
studied across function-level benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP), competitive
programming (CodeContests), and repository repair alike.

\subsection{Intent Capture: The Open-Loop Stage}
\label{sec:intent}

Intent enters as a GitHub issue, a bug report, or a sentence typed into a chat
box. It is the only stage of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} that no surveyed system
instruments, and the asymmetry is not obviously the right design.

\paragraph{Intent quality dominates outcomes.}
The clearest evidence comes from the benchmarks. SWE-bench Verified was built by
having human engineers discard instances whose issue text was
\emph{underspecified}, that is, where the intent did not determine the
patch~\cite{openai2024verified}. Screening the intent, while changing neither the
repositories, the agents, nor the grading harness, raises resolve rates by tens
of points (\S\ref{sec:e2e-eval}). The same lever appears with the opposite sign
in Lite, where some issue descriptions leak the fix outright, so that the intent
contains its own solution~\cite{xia2024agentless}. An agent's score is, to a
degree rarely acknowledged, a score on the issue-writing of the repository it was
sampled from.

\paragraph{No end-to-end agent closes the loop here.}
Every other stage regulates on an external signal: planning against repository
structure, generation against a parser and a linter, execution against a test
runner, refinement against the resulting failures. Intent capture, as the
surveyed systems implement it, has none. None of the five systems of
\S\ref{sec:e2e-systems} asks a clarifying question when the issue text is
ambiguous; each commits to a reading and proceeds. OpenHands and Devin admit
human interjection, but the initiative is the human's. In control terms the stage
is \emph{open-loop}: the model's reading of the issue simply \emph{is} the
specification, unchallenged.

The mechanism is not unknown. TiCoder~\cite{lahiri2022ticoder} formalises intent
through generated tests the user accepts or rejects;
ClarifyGPT~\cite{mu2024clarifygpt} detects ambiguity by testing whether the
model's own completions disagree, and asks only when they do; and Vijayvargiya et
al.~\cite{vijayvargiya2026ambiguity} carry the idea to repository scale, building
an underspecified variant of SWE-bench Verified on which an agent that queries a
simulated user substantially outperforms one that guesses. They also find that
frontier models left alone fail to distinguish specified from underspecified
requests, and default to assuming rather than asking. The gap is therefore one of
\emph{transfer, not invention}. A clarifying question put to a human is an
external signal in exactly the sense of \S\ref{sec:feedback-signals}: the human
can contradict the model, and no other stage's oracle can.

Why the omission persists is a question the benchmark answers. A dataset of
issues that were, by construction, resolved by a real merged PR is a dataset in
which the intent was adequate, and Verified deliberately removed the instances
where it was not. An agent is never rewarded for asking, and never penalised for
guessing wrong about a genuinely ambiguous request. That the clarification
literature had to construct a \emph{new} benchmark~\cite{vijayvargiya2026ambiguity}
before the stage could be measured at all is the strongest evidence that the
omission is an artefact of evaluation rather than of difficulty.

The simplest plan representation is chain of thought~\cite{wei2022chain}: a linear
sequence that commits early to one trajectory, so that if the first design choice
is wrong, subsequent steps elaborate the mistake. Tree of
Thoughts~\cite{yao2023tree} and Graph of Thoughts~\cite{besta2024graph} relax
this by allowing exploration, backtracking, and merging
(Figure~\ref{fig:plan-shapes}). The mechanism in Tree of Thoughts is worth naming
precisely, since the figure's ``backtrack'' arrow understates it: the tree is
expanded under a classical search policy in which an LLM \emph{value function}
scores each partial state, so branches are pruned by lookahead rather than
abandoned once they fail. The evaluator, not the branching, is what makes the
structure useful, and it is the component a repository-scale agent finds hardest
to supply: what is a half-finished patch worth, before any test has run? The
difficulty is cost rather than impossibility.
SWE-Search~\cite{antoniades2024swesearch} builds such an evaluator, a hybrid LLM
value function scoring partial trajectories inside a Monte Carlo tree search, for
a 23\% relative improvement; that it must invoke a language model at every node is
also why it has not displaced the cheaper pipelines of \S\ref{sec:e2e-systems}.

Repository-level agents add a more concrete representation:
\emph{localisation plans}. Agentless decomposes issue resolution into files,
classes or functions, and edit locations before generating a
patch~\cite{xia2024agentless}; AutoCodeRover plans over program structure through
AST-backed search APIs~\cite{zhang2024autocoderover}. These grounded
representations name concrete program elements that can be checked against the
repository, turning a broad intent into a bounded search problem. Plans may also
be expressed as \emph{specification sketches}: partial interfaces, pseudocode,
assertions, or examples. What the useful ones share is that they are grounded in
observable artefacts, not invented requirements.

\subsection{Search and Exploration Strategies}

Once a representation exists, an agent must decide how broadly to search.
Single-sample generation treats the model's first completion as the solution;
search-based systems spend additional inference to explore alternatives. The
simplest form is self-consistency: sample multiple reasoning paths and select the
most common answer~\cite{wang2023selfconsistency}. Transposing this to code forces
the idea to mutate, and the reason is instructive. Self-consistency marginalises
over reasoning paths that terminate in a \emph{single canonical answer}, so votes
can be counted by string equality. Programs have no canonical form: two correct
patches may share no token, and two textually near-identical patches may differ
semantically. The vote must therefore be taken in a different space. Systems
recover it either \emph{behaviourally}, by clustering candidates on their outputs
over a set of inputs (AlphaCode~\cite{li2022alphacode},
CodeT~\cite{chen2022codet}), or \emph{syntactically up to normalisation}, by
comparing candidates after AST canonicalisation
(Agentless~\cite{xia2024agentless}). Sample-and-rank is thus self-consistency with
an equivalence relation borrowed from program semantics rather than from string
matching, and the quality of that relation bounds what the vote can buy.

AlphaCode generated up to millions of candidates for competitive programming,
filtered and clustered them by execution results, then submitted a small diverse
set; CodeT generates both programs and tests, selecting code consistent with the
generated tests. The key insight is that LLMs often contain the right solution
somewhere in the sample distribution even when the top-ranked output is wrong.
The limitation is self-referential objectives: if the generated tests are weak,
selection optimises for the model's own misunderstanding. Execution-guided search
is strongest when the ranking signal is an external oracle, and it degrades
smoothly toward noise as that signal is replaced by a model's own artefacts.

Search also appears in repository navigation, and there it is not over complete
programs but over \emph{where to look} and \emph{what evidence to include}: a
correct patch is unlikely if the agent never places the relevant function,
invariant, or failing trace in context. Its cost is the trade-off, since more
samples multiply latency, tokens, and validation. Search is most useful when the
ranking signal is at least as reliable as the generator is diverse.

\subsection{Plan--Generate Interplay}

Planning and generation can be coupled in three ways.
\emph{Plan-then-generate} plans once and conditions generation on the result: easy
to inspect, brittle if the plan is wrong~\cite{xia2024agentless}.
\emph{Interleaved planning} alternates plan updates and tool use inside the
observe--think--act loop~\cite{yao2023react}: adaptive, but the plan is
distributed across many turns and is hard to audit. \emph{Generate-then-refine}
treats the first output as a hypothesis and revises it via critique or execution
feedback~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine}, which connects planning to
\S\ref{sec:refinement}.

The central design question is not whether agents should plan, but how plans
remain \emph{grounded}. Grounding mechanisms include retrieval over repository
structure, AST-level symbol search, execution traces, failing tests, and explicit
constraints extracted from the issue. Effective systems keep plans close to these
artefacts and treat generation as one move in a larger search, reframing code
synthesis from ``write the answer'' to ``maintain and test a hypothesis about the
required program change''.

This distinction resolves what would otherwise be an embarrassment. Agentless, the
system that plans \emph{least}, is competitive with those that plan most
(\S\ref{sec:e2e-systems}). If the claim were that deliberation helps, Agentless
would refute it. The claim is instead that \emph{grounding} helps, and
deliberation is merely one way to obtain it, often an expensive one. Agentless
obtains grounding structurally, by hard-coding the hierarchical narrowing that an
agentic loop must rediscover through exploration, and it therefore confirms the
thesis rather than embarrassing it. The corollary is that finding the right
location is harder than editing it once found: AutoCodeRover's SBFL variant
changes nothing about repair, only about localisation, and buys three points of
resolve rate together with seven instances no other configuration
reaches~\cite{zhang2024autocoderover}. Planning here is not reasoning about
\emph{how} to write code. It is reasoning about \emph{where} the code is, and
about what evidence would confirm that guess.

\section{Execution Feedback and Iterative Refinement}
\label{sec:refinement}

Code generation rarely succeeds in one shot. Agentic systems execute generated
code, observe runtime outcomes, and revise. While \S\ref{sec:e2e} scores agents on
SWE-bench, refinement mechanisms are often evaluated on function-level suites
(HumanEval, MBPP), text-to-SQL (Spider), and APR benchmarks (Defects4J).

One distinction organises the whole section: \emph{what closes the loop}. A
refinement mechanism is a control loop only if some signal outside the model's own
judgement can contradict the model. The literature usually reads this as a binary,
external feedback against self-critique, but that binary has no room for the
mechanism carrying most of the field's recent gains, so we distinguish three
positions. An \emph{authoritative oracle} (a test runner, a compiler, a type
checker, a human) is ground truth: it can surprise the model, and refinement
against it reliably improves code. A \emph{learned or generated proxy} (a trained
critic, model-generated tests, an LLM judge) stands one step removed. It carries
real information, because ground truth entered it at training or generation time,
and that is why it works; but at inference it is a model, not an oracle, and it
cannot surprise anything. Its ceiling is the fidelity of the proxy, which is
exactly the bound \S\ref{sec:planning} derived for sample-and-rank. Finally,
\emph{same-distribution self-critique} draws the critic from the very distribution
that produced the error, so the loop closes onto itself and the apparent control
is illusory. Table~\ref{tab:loop-closure} sorts the mechanisms on this axis, and
\S\ref{sec:refinement-limits} supplies the evidence that the axis is the one that
matters. The split earns its keep immediately: the two most-cited positive results
below, Reflexion's HumanEval headline and OpenHands's inference-time scaling, both
sit in the middle column, and reading them as oracle-closed overstates what they
show.

\subsection{Feedback Signals and Environments}
\label{sec:feedback-signals}

Agents consume five broad signals, and the useful way to sort them is not by form
but by whether they can tell the model something it does not already believe.
\emph{Test results} and \emph{compiler or interpreter errors} are oracles: they
are computed by an artefact outside the model and are indifferent to its
confidence. \emph{Static analysis} is likewise oracular and unusually cheap,
because it reports before execution. \emph{Human feedback} is the most
authoritative signal and the least available. \emph{Self-critique}, in which the
model explains its own code and looks for discrepancies (``rubber duck
debugging'')~\cite{chen2024selfdebug}, is the only one of the five generated by
the system under test.

An oracle is authoritative because it can \emph{surprise}: a failing test
contradicts the model's belief regardless of how confident that belief was. It
requires infrastructure and may be slow. Self-critique is fast and needs no setup,
but it is generated by the same distribution that produced the error, so it
inherits the error's blind spots. An agent that could reliably tell its wrong code
from its right code by inspection would have produced the right code in the first
place. Between them sits the proxy: a generated test suite or a trained critic is
a model artefact, so it cannot surprise, yet it is fitted against ground truth the
generator cannot consult at inference, so it can carry information the generator
lacks. Its characteristic failure is specific: where a true oracle is wrong only
if the test suite is wrong, a proxy is wrong exactly where the model was wrong to
begin with, which is where it is needed most.

\subsection{Iterative Refinement Mechanisms}
\label{sec:refinement-mechanisms}

\paragraph{Reflexion.}
Reflexion~\cite{shinn2023reflexion} treats refinement as reinforcement learning
without weight updates. After each trial the agent generates a natural-language
critique of what went wrong, stores it in an episodic memory buffer, and retrieves
it on subsequent attempts. The innovation is converting feedback into
\emph{verbal} form that persists across trials. On HumanEval, Reflexion reports
91\% pass@1 against 80\% for single-shot GPT-4. It is worth being precise about
what closes the loop in that number, because it is routinely misread. Reflexion is
usually described as learning from an \emph{external} reward, and on tasks that
supply one it does; on HumanEval no such reward exists at inference, so Reflexion
writes its own unit tests and reflects on their verdicts. The signal is a
generated proxy, and it inherits precisely the failure
Table~\ref{tab:loop-closure} assigns to Agentless's generated tests.

\paragraph{Self-debugging.}
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2024selfdebug} have the model explain its own code
line-by-line, so that the explanation serves as a self-generated specification
against which discrepancies become visible; where execution feedback is available,
the model also reasons about error messages before proposing fixes. On Spider,
where no unit tests are available, self-debugging improves accuracy by 2--3\%; on
benchmarks that supply tests (TransCoder, MBPP) the reported improvement reaches
12\%. The gap between those numbers is the point. A small but real gain survives
without any oracle, so pure self-critique is not worthless; a gain several times
larger appears as soon as an oracle is admitted.

\paragraph{How the edit is emitted.}
How a revision is \emph{expressed} is an action-interface decision
(\S\ref{sec:e2e-arch}) with consequences for the loop. SWE-agent's \texttt{edit}
command takes a line range, so the model must track line numbers across edits.
Agentless instead emits a \emph{search/replace} block quoting the source text to
be replaced, anchoring the edit to content rather than position, specifically
because that leaves fewer opportunities for hallucination than line arithmetic
does. The shared trade-off is that a parse failure breaks the loop.

\subsection{Does Refinement Work? The Limits of Self-Correction}
\label{sec:refinement-limits}

The results above are usually read as evidence that agents improve their own code.
Three lines of work complicate that reading, and taking them seriously sharpens
rather than weakens the control-layer thesis.

\paragraph{Self-repair is partly inference-time scaling in disguise.}
Olausson et al.~\cite{olausson2024selfrepair} observe that a self-repair loop does
not spend a fixed budget: producing a critique and a revision consumes tokens that
could instead have drawn more independent candidates. Normalising for that budget,
against simply sampling $k$ programs i.i.d.\ and taking the best, much of
self-repair's apparent gain disappears. What survives is attributable to the
\emph{feedback}, not the iteration: repair helps most when the model receives a
stronger critique than it can produce for itself, and in their strongest condition
the critique comes from a human. Reflexion's HumanEval headline should therefore
be read as a result about a \emph{budget} as much as a mechanism, and compared
against a best-of-$N$ baseline at matched cost.

\paragraph{Intrinsic self-correction does not reliably help.}
Huang et al.~\cite{huang2024selfcorrect} isolate the case where no external signal
is available and find that models asked to revise their own answers often make
them worse, turning correct answers into incorrect ones somewhat more often than
the reverse. Their evidence is on reasoning benchmarks (GSM8K, CommonSenseQA,
HotpotQA) rather than on code, and we flag that rather than quietly generalising:
what transfers is not the measurement but the mechanism. The critic and the
generator are the same distribution, so the critique cannot introduce information
the generator lacked, and nothing in that argument is specific to arithmetic. On
code, the load is carried by Olausson et al.

\paragraph{The constructive converse.}
A thesis resting only on what breaks is weaker than one that also predicts what
works. CRITIC~\cite{gou2024critic} supplies the missing half: holding the model
fixed, self-critique that may consult an external tool corrects reliably, while
self-critique that may not yields little or nothing. The variable that moves the
outcome is neither the model nor the prompt but the presence of something outside
the model in the loop, which is the axis of Table~\ref{tab:loop-closure} isolated
experimentally. Its own limit is instructive: a tool helps only for errors it can
detect, the same coverage bound weak test suites impose in \S\ref{sec:convergence}.

\paragraph{What this implies.}
Refinement is a control layer when a signal the generator did not produce closes
the loop, and yields only small and unreliable gains when it does not. Every
mechanism that survives contact with repository-scale evaluation (Agentless's
validation phase, SWE-agent's edit--execute loop, D4C's test-driven repair) is
externally closed; the purely internal ones cluster in function-level benchmarks
where a comprehensive suite is nonetheless available to the \emph{evaluator}, if
not to the agent.

This also unifies two techniques the literature treats separately. Iterative
self-repair and best-of-$N$ with a reranker are the same lever, a budget of extra
inference spent to reduce variance, pulled sequentially or in parallel. Once
budget is fixed the question is not ``does the agent refine?'' but ``which
allocation of the same tokens buys more correctness?'' OpenHands answers in one
direction: five parallel rollouts reranked by a dedicated critic reach 66.4\% on
Verified where a single rollout reaches 60.6\%~\cite{openhands2025verified}. The
critic is a \emph{separate, trained} model rather than the generator judging
itself, and that separation is what makes the gain possible. But it is a proxy,
not an oracle, so this is not simply ``external feedback wins'': Olausson et al.'s
strongest condition was a human critic, and the gap here is bought by ranking
rollouts that were themselves executed, that is, by an oracle and a proxy
together.

\subsection{Fault Localisation and Self-Repair}
\label{sec:fault-localization}

When refinement targets bugs in existing code rather than draft solutions, it
becomes \emph{automated program repair} (APR), classically staged as fault
localisation, patch generation, and validation. Agents challenge this structure.
RepairAgent~\cite{bouzenia2025repairagent} shows that the observe--think--act loop
transfers wholesale: driven by a finite-state machine over a small tool set, it
localises, edits, and tests without a separate localisation phase, repairing
164~Defects4J bugs of which 39 had resisted every prior technique.

Pre-LLM APR relies on \emph{spectrum-based fault localisation} (SBFL), ranking
lines by suspiciousness derived from test coverage (Ochiai, Tarantula, DStar). But
even oracle-provided bug locations do not guarantee correct repairs.
D4C~\cite{xu2024d4c} argues that LLMs should repair entire functions rather than
isolated hunks, aligning the task with their infilling pre-training objective: on
Defects4J it repairs 180 single-function bugs with 10 samples each, outperforming
systems with perfect fault localisation by 10\%.

\paragraph{Reconciling FL-free repair with localisation-first agents.}
Read beside \S\ref{sec:planning}, D4C appears to contradict the systems surveyed
there: Agentless and AutoCodeRover treat localisation as the core of the problem,
while D4C reports it is unnecessary and even harmful. Both are right, because they
operate in different regimes, and naming the regime is more useful than picking a
winner. D4C's Defects4J tasks are \emph{single-function} bugs: the buggy function
is supplied. What D4C eliminates is therefore not the search for the faulty
function but the finer-grained ranking of \emph{lines within} a function already
known to be faulty, and its argument, that line-level targeting fights the model's
infilling prior, is convincing at that granularity. A SWE-bench agent faces a
prior question, which function among tens of thousands, and no pre-training prior
answers it: the relevant symbol may not appear in the issue text at all. The
reconciliation is a claim about scale. \emph{Below} the function boundary,
localisation is a constraint the model does not need and should not be given;
\emph{above} it, localisation is the task. This is why AutoCodeRover's SBFL variant
helps (it ranks functions) while D4C's ablation of perfect FL helps (it un-ranks
lines): the two results, superficially opposed, are measuring opposite sides of
the same boundary.

One special case decides whether localisation is a problem at all. Where the fault
crashes, the stack trace localises it for free, and systems such as
SelfEvolve~\cite{jiang2023selfevolve} simply feed the trace back to the model.
Where it does not crash, as with an off-by-one that silently returns the wrong
answer, no trace exists, the oracle reports only a failed assertion, and the agent
is thrown back on search. That is why an interpreter is a weaker oracle than a
test suite.

\subsection{Convergence and Stopping Criteria}
\label{sec:convergence}

Iterative refinement must terminate, balancing quality, cost, and the risk of
overfitting to weak test suites.

The simplest criterion is \emph{full test pass}. This works for competitive
programming, where suites are comprehensive, but in repository tasks tests may be
incomplete: a patch that passes all tests may still be wrong, and a correct patch
may fail on environment issues. The criterion is also, quietly, an oracle. A loop
that halts exactly when the answer becomes right has smuggled ground truth into
its stopping rule, and any self-critique operating inside such a loop will appear
more effective than it is.

A second criterion is \emph{diminishing improvement}: if iterations 3, 4, and 5 all
fail the same test with the same error, further iteration is unlikely to help. The
empirical basis is the shape of the repair curve. Olausson et al.\ find the
marginal return of an additional repair round falls off quickly and, once the token
budget is held fixed, is frequently negative relative to drawing a fresh
independent sample~\cite{olausson2024selfrepair}; where the loop is closed only
internally, Huang et al.\ find it can be negative in absolute
terms~\cite{huang2024selfcorrect}. A stopping rule and a budget allocation are
therefore the same decision, and ``iterate until it passes'' is defensible only
when an external oracle defines \emph{passes}. Because sequential refinement and
parallel best-of-$N$ draw on the same pool of tokens, fixing the budget is what
makes the two strategies comparable at all, and an iteration cap is implicitly a
bet about which converts tokens into correctness more efficiently.

\paragraph{Overfitting and the human oracle.}
If the suite is weak, the agent may generate code that narrowly passes it and fails
on unseen inputs: the ``plausible patch'' problem. The mitigations sort onto the
axis of \S\ref{sec:feedback-signals}, and the sorting is more useful than the list.
Strengthening the oracle genuinely helps: static checks add a second, independent
artefact that must also be satisfied, and they are cheap. Strengthening the
\emph{proxy} helps less: test augmentation inherits whatever the generator
misunderstood, which is precisely where the extra tests are needed. And asking the
model to predict whether its own patch generalises is not a mitigation but the open
loop of \S\ref{sec:refinement-limits} wearing a new hat. No technique eliminates
overfitting, and the reason is structural: the oracle is a proxy for correctness,
and optimising against a proxy is what overfitting \emph{is}. Where no automated
oracle suffices, the remaining move is to insert a human as the oracle, as
OpenHands and Devin both allow; \S\ref{sec:open} argues this is less a solution
than a restatement of the problem.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We now consolidate what the three fronts share and which constraints bound all of
them. Progress has come less from any single architectural idea than from
\emph{coupling} generation to external evidence, and that same coupling exposes
the field's hardest problems: evaluation integrity, finite context, and cost.

\subsection{Consolidated Patterns and Trends}

\paragraph{Three recurring patterns, and what unites them.}
The \emph{observe--think--act loop}~\cite{yao2023react} is the near-universal
control structure (\S\ref{sec:e2e-arch}), Agentless's fixed
pipeline~\cite{xia2024agentless} being the exception that stays competitive by
hard-coding the sequence the loop would otherwise discover.
\emph{Execution-grounded selection}, which ranks candidates by tests or regression
preservation rather than by model confidence (AlphaCode~\cite{li2022alphacode},
CodeT~\cite{chen2022codet}, Agentless), is the most reliable quality lever. And
\emph{planning acts on generation} rather than preceding it
(\S\ref{sec:planning}). The second pattern explains the other two. Everything that
works, works by putting something outside the model in a position to contradict
it: a test runner, a compiler, an AST index, a human. Planning earns its place by
grounding hypotheses in repository structure that can be checked; refinement earns
its place when a signal the generator did not produce closes the loop, and yields
only small and unreliable gains when it does not, gains that on reasoning tasks
turn outright negative (\S\ref{sec:refinement-limits}). Read this way the pipeline is not five stages of
reasoning but a scaffold for arranging confrontations between a generative model
and an environment that can say no, and its two weakest points are exactly the two
places where nothing can: intent capture, whose available oracle is a human nobody
queries (\S\ref{sec:intent}), and test-based validation, whose oracle is weak
enough to be gamed (\S\ref{sec:eval-landscape}).

\paragraph{Agents regulate tightly on a proxy setpoint.}
Calling both stages a ``control layer'' compresses two things, and the difference
sharpens the thesis. Refinement is a control loop literally: a signal is measured
against a setpoint and the output revised. Planning is not. Agentless's
hierarchical narrowing regulates against nothing; it is a feed-forward pass whose
contribution is to make the hypothesis \emph{checkable} rather than to check it.
What unites them is not feedback but the placement of an external artefact between
the model and its own confidence. And the setpoint that refinement does regulate
against, ``the tests pass,'' is a leaky proxy: patches that pass can be overfitted
or the product of reward hacking (\S\ref{sec:convergence}). Current agents are
therefore control loops regulating tightly on a signal that only approximates what
we want, and their impressive convergence and their characteristic failures are
the same phenomenon viewed from either end.

\paragraph{Macro trends, and their expiry date.}
The field has moved from single-shot prompting to agentic scaffolds, from flat
chain-of-thought to structured search and localisation, and most recently to
\emph{inference-time scaling}, where trajectories are sampled and reranked by a
critic (OpenHands's 60.6\%~$\to$~66.4\%~\cite{openhands2025verified}). The
consistent finding across \S\ref{sec:e2e}, that \emph{scaffold and interface
matter as much as the backbone model}, is time-stamped rather than timeless: a
newer line of work moves the scaffold's competence into the weights, by
reinforcement learning over software-evolution data~\cite{wei2025swerl} or by
training agent and verifier together in an executable
environment~\cite{pan2024swegym}. If this succeeds, the scaffolding advantage is a
transitional artefact of using models never trained to be agents. Deployment has
shifted in parallel: the systems surveyed here are research artefacts, while the
agents developers use (Claude Code, Cursor, Codex) are products whose scaffolds
are undocumented and whose evaluations are self-reported, which is one reason the
integrity questions below have become urgent.

\subsection{The Evaluation Landscape}
\label{sec:eval-landscape}
\paragraph{A layered benchmark stack, straining at every layer.}
\emph{Function-level} suites (HumanEval, MBPP) are effectively saturated
(Reflexion reported 91\% pass@1 on HumanEval already in
2023~\cite{shinn2023reflexion}) and no longer discriminate among frontier systems.
Part of that saturation is contamination rather than capability:
LiveCodeBench~\cite{jain2025livecodebench}, which admits only problems published
after a model's training cutoff, finds performance drops on genuinely unseen
problems, the same story Figure~\ref{fig:verified-vs-pro} tells one level up.
\emph{Repository-level} evaluation shifted the centre of gravity to
SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}, whose Verified
split~\cite{openai2024verified} is the de-facto standard, yet \S\ref{sec:e2e-eval}
already noted its difficulty skew (39\% of tasks are trivial $<$15-min fixes and
86\% touch a single file~\cite{ganhotra2025difficulty}), its repository monoculture
(Django dominates), and its contamination risk~\cite{epoch2025swebench}.

\paragraph{Three responses have emerged.}
The first is \emph{harder, contamination-resistant successors}. SWE-bench
Pro~\cite{deng2025swebenchpro} contributes 1{,}865 long-horizon problems from 41
repositories, resisting memorisation with copyleft and held-out proprietary code;
at release, top systems scored $\sim$23\% against 70\%+ on Verified.
SWE-bench Multilingual~\cite{swebench2025multilingual} extends the format to 300
tasks across 9 languages. Figure~\ref{fig:verified-vs-pro} makes the divergence
concrete: models near or above 80\% on Verified fall to the mid-40s--high-50s on
Pro, Claude Opus~4.5 scoring 80.9\% against 45.9\% under Scale's standardised
harness~\cite{deng2025swebenchpro,agentmarketcap2026gap}. Two confounds prevent
reading the \emph{size} of each drop as a memorisation measurement, and we flag
them because they are the confounds this report holds others to: Pro is harder as
well as fresher, and its column mixes harnesses, only Opus~4.5 being scored under
Scale's standardised evaluation. Consistency then demands that we decline the
quantity the figure invites us to quote. The width of the band \emph{is} the
per-model spread we have just said cannot be read, and its upper end belongs to
the one model measured most strictly. What survives is a floor and a direction:
every model drops by at least twenty points, and no mechanism that would explain a
drop of that size is flattering. The signal was strong enough that OpenAI stopped
reporting Verified in early 2026, citing near-universal contamination (models
reproduce gold patches from the task ID alone) and flawed residual
tests~\cite{openai2026swebench}.

The second response targets \emph{benchmark integrity directly}: a stricter
harness that closes test-leakage channels cuts reported scores substantially
(double-digit drops on Pro for some models), prompting the observation that
``reward hacking is swamping model intelligence gains'', that agents partly learn
to exploit the grading oracle rather than solve the
task~\cite{cursor2025rewardhacking}. The third is \emph{efficiency-aware
evaluation}: SWE-Effi~\cite{sweeffi2025} re-scores agents under resource
constraints and finds resolve rate decoupling from cost. Agentless leads its study
on resolve rate (48\% with Qwen3-32B) but consumes 83.1 API calls and 209.4
seconds per issue, so a single pass@1 number conceals an order-of-magnitude spread
in cost.

\paragraph{One number no longer suffices.}
Credible comparison now requires difficulty stratification, contamination
controls, and cost normalisation reported alongside accuracy. The pipeline framing
makes the coverage gap concrete: most benchmarks score only the \emph{end} of the
pipeline (did the final patch pass?), leaving plan quality, localisation
precision, and refinement efficiency unmeasured.

\subsection{The Context Window Bottleneck}
\paragraph{Longer windows do not dissolve the bottleneck.}
Every stage is constrained by the same resource: finite working context. Real
repositories exceed even million-token windows, so an agent never sees the whole
program and must decide \emph{what} to read, and longer windows do not settle the
question. Repository-level studies find that retaining the full \emph{dependency}
context works best, while enlarging context with loosely related code can mislead
and degrade correctness~\cite{repoexec2025}: more tokens are not uniformly better.
A second failure mode is dynamic. Agents that append every observation to an
ever-growing trajectory suffer \emph{context explosion} and \emph{semantic drift},
as critical early information is buried~\cite{contextastool2025}. Cost compounds
with it: because the whole prefix is re-sent each turn, tokens processed over $n$
turns grow as $O(n^2)$, which is why turn budgets are an efficiency lever and not
merely a safety one~\cite{turncontrol2025}. That exponent is a property of the
protocol rather than an empirical finding, and prompt caching (used by every
deployed agent) cuts the constant by roughly an order of magnitude without
changing the growth rate.

\paragraph{Context as a managed resource.}
The surveyed systems therefore treat context as a managed resource rather than a
passive log: hierarchical repository skeletons with embedding retrieval
(Agentless), AST search returning a class skeleton before any full method body
(AutoCodeRover), and observation compression of older turns (SWE-agent). The
current direction is to make context management \emph{active}, summarising,
pruning, and re-retrieving as a deliberate agent
action~\cite{contextastool2025}. Context quality, not model capability alone,
frequently determines whether the correct patch is even reachable: an agent cannot
fix what it never places in context.

\subsection{Open Challenges}
\label{sec:open}
Three challenges cut across all three fronts and remain open.

\paragraph{Long-horizon, multi-file reasoning.}
Agents excel at small, local, single-file fixes but degrade steeply on tasks
requiring coordinated changes across many files. On Verified the best single
system resolves about 80\% of the easy tier but only 24\% of the
hard~\cite{ganhotra2025difficulty}, and the drop on the explicitly long-horizon
SWE-bench Pro~\cite{deng2025swebenchpro} points at the same ceiling.

Is this the same constraint as the context bottleneck above, or a distinct one?
\S\ref{sec:e2e-eval} showed that the easy and medium tiers behave like an
\emph{ensembling} problem, the union over nine systems running far ahead of any
one of them, while the hard tier does not. That contrast rules out a
\emph{sampling} limitation. It does not rule out retrieval: the nine systems share
the issue text and a narrow family of localisation strategies, and three of them
are the same SWE-agent scaffold at different backbones, so a blind spot common to
all of them would look identical to a reasoning ceiling.

We nonetheless expect the hard tier to be moved by training-time
approaches~\cite{wei2025swerl,pan2024swegym} rather than by retrieval, since hard
instances are disproportionately multi-file (only 9 of 25 such issues are solved
by anyone) and composing a coherent cross-file change is not what more context
supplies. To make that a prediction rather than an expectation it needs a
discriminating test, and one is available: hold the retrieval pipeline fixed and
give the agent the gold file set, so that localisation is oracular by
construction. If hard-tier rates then rise substantially, the ceiling was
retrieval and we are wrong; if they do not, only training will move it. We are not
aware of anyone having run this ablation, and it is the cheapest experiment we can
name that would settle a question the field currently answers by assertion, ours
included.

\paragraph{Specification ambiguity.}
This is the open-loop intent stage of \S\ref{sec:intent}, seen from the outside.
Agents start from natural-language intent that is often underspecified. When the issue text omits edge cases or implicit invariants, the
agent must guess, and execution feedback only constrains behaviour that the
available tests exercise, so a wrong guess about an untested invariant is never
contradicted and is scored as success. Closing the gap between an ambiguous
intent and a verified specification is not an unstudied problem
(\S\ref{sec:intent}): clarification, test synthesis and interactive intent
capture all exist and all
work~\cite{lahiri2022ticoder,mu2024clarifygpt,vijayvargiya2026ambiguity}. What is
missing is adoption. No end-to-end system surveyed here queries a user, and the
incentive to add one is absent by construction, because a dataset built from
issues that a real pull request resolved is a dataset from which ambiguity has
been filtered out in advance. The challenge is therefore not to invent the
mechanism but to build an evaluation that would reward using it.

\paragraph{Cost and efficiency.}
The dominant quality lever, inference-time scaling with critic reranking, directly
multiplies cost, while trajectory length inflates tokens per
turn~\cite{turncontrol2025}. Because resolve rate decouples from
compute~\cite{sweeffi2025}, the practical frontier is not peak accuracy but
accuracy per dollar and per minute.

\subsection{Implications for Software Engineering Practice}
\label{sec:practice}
These trends shift the developer's role rather than remove it: as agents absorb
localisation, editing, and test-driven repair, the human contribution moves
upstream (precise intent and acceptance criteria) and downstream (reviewing and
accepting patches). First, \emph{verification becomes the bottleneck}. With
benchmark scores partly reflecting reward hacking~\cite{cursor2025rewardhacking}
and validation oracles weak enough to admit plausible-but-wrong patches
(\S\ref{sec:convergence}), a green test run is not acceptance, and stronger
oracles (regression suites, human review) become first-class pipeline stages. The
cost of that review is easy to underestimate and has now been measured: in a
randomised controlled trial,
experienced open-source developers took 19\% \emph{longer} on their own
repositories when allowed to use early-2025 AI tools, while estimating afterwards
that the tools had made them 20\% faster~\cite{metr2025rct}. One study on a narrow
population settles nothing, but the direction of the error is instructive: the
generation speed-up is visible and the verification burden is not, so the people
best placed to notice the trade did not. Second, \emph{execution infrastructure
becomes central}: sandboxes, reproducible environments, and CI integration are
what make execution-grounded selection possible at all. The economic frame shifts
with them, from ``can an agent resolve this issue?'' to ``at what cost, and with
what assurance?''

\section{Conclusion}
This report surveyed autonomous AI agents for code generation through a single
lens, the five-stage agent pipeline, and synthesised three research fronts that
instantiate its stages. End-to-end systems (\S\ref{sec:e2e}) converge on an
observe--think--act loop over a purpose-built agent--computer interface, with
repository-scale context managed by retrieval, AST search, or compression; that
Agentless rivals interactive agents at lower cost shows autonomy is a design
choice, not a prerequisite. Planning (\S\ref{sec:planning}) contributes not
deliberation but \emph{grounding}, which is why the least deliberative system
remains competitive. Execution feedback (\S\ref{sec:refinement}) shows that
running the code, not generating it, supplies most of the reliability.

The pipeline framing makes the central coupling visible, and sharpens it into
something testable: planning and refinement keep generation tethered to external
evidence, but only where a signal the generator did not produce can contradict it.
Between the oracles that can and the self-critique that cannot lies the proxy, a
trained critic or a generated test, which carries real information yet is bounded
by its own fidelity (\S\ref{sec:refinement-limits}). Much of what the field
reports as successful self-improvement lives in that middle band. Read this way,
the pipeline's two weaknesses are the two stages with no oracle behind them:
intent capture, whose only oracle is a human nobody queries (\S\ref{sec:intent}),
and validation, whose oracle is weak enough to be gamed
(\S\ref{sec:eval-landscape}). The question at the frontier has changed
accordingly, from ``can an agent resolve real issues?'' to ``at what cost, and how
much can the result be trusted?''

The promising directions each amount to supplying an oracle where one is missing:
contamination-resistant benchmarks reported with cost and difficulty
stratification, and benchmarks that score the pipeline's intermediate stages
rather than only its final patch; a decision, by the ablation of \S\ref{sec:open},
on whether long-horizon reasoning is a training problem or a retrieval one; the
adoption at repository scale of the interactive intent capture that already works
at function level. Agentic code generation has clearly crossed from autocomplete
to autonomous issue resolution. Whether it crosses from
impressive to dependable will be decided by whether we learn to let the
environment say no to the model as reliably as a reader once did, because on the
evidence of this report the model will not say it to itself.
